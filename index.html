Introduction

The Silueta series (1973–1980) comprises performances in which Ana Mendieta imprinted or outlined her silhouette into natural elements such as sand, earth, rock or fire. Working from the perspective of a Cuban exile informed by Afro‑Cuban and indigenous traditions, she sought to reconnect with land and ancestral cosmologies. Her earth‑body works challenge imperial scopic regimes— systems of seeing and representation that link observation to knowledge and power. By dissolving her body into landscape, Mendieta resists the colonising gaze that historically positioned the European subject as classifier and possessor of other beings. Limitations

  • The Silueta actions survive primarily through photographs and films; documenting them freezes events meant to vanish, reintroducing the camera’s frame and potentially reinscribing the imperial scopic regime.
  • Associations between women and nature risk reinforcing essentialist or voyeuristic tropes; the female body may still be objectified despite the artist’s intention to dissolve it.
  • Using sacred or protected sites and indigenous rituals blurs homage and appropriation; context can be lost when works are detached from the communities that inform them.
  • Exhibiting the works within Western institutions situates them inside structures of power they aim to critique, influencing how audiences read them.
  • Materials such as blood, fire and vivid pigments may draw attention to spectacle rather than to the decolonial critique embedded in the actions.
